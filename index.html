Labour Force in Malaysia

Labour Force Participation by State

From 2019 to 2023, Malaysia's Labour Force Participation Rate varied across states. Urban areas like Selangor and Penang consistently showed higher participation, while rural regions had lower engagement.

Employment grew steadily from 15.25 million in 2019 to 16.35 million in 2023, with Selangor leading, followed by Sabah and Sarawak. Unemployment remained stable, increasing slightly from 512,000 to 565,000, with fluctuations during the COVID-19 pandemic, especially in Sabah.

The number of people outside the labour force, including students and retirees, remained high, increasing from 7.07 million to 7.20 million during this period. Overall, Malaysia saw steady employment growth with slight unemployment fluctuations, while a significant portion of the population stayed outside the labour force due to socioeconomic factors.

Employment

Employment Growth and Trends by Industry

The chart shows the relationship between total hours worked, labour productivity, and the employed labour force across industries from 2019 to 2023. Key sectors like Manufacturing and Services dominate, while smaller sectors like Mining and Quarrying contribute less.

Manufacturing sectors such as Electrical & Optical Products and Wood, Furniture & Printing exhibit high productivity despite smaller workforces, especially in 2022-2023. In contrast, Food & Accommodation and Transport & Storage have lower productivity despite longer working hours, indicating potential inefficiencies.

The Services sector employs the largest workforce, particularly in Wholesale & Retail Trade. Meanwhile, Agriculture has a moderate workforce with relatively lower productivity, and Construction shows steady growth in both workforce size and productivity. Overall, high-tech industries in Manufacturing and Services are vital for driving productivity improvements in Malaysia's labour market.

Gender Distribution of Employed Labour Force by Industry

The chart shows the gender distribution of the employed labor force across different sectors. In Agriculture, males slightly outnumber females, but the gap is smaller compared to other sectors. Mining and Quarrying is overwhelmingly male-dominated, with minimal female representation. Similarly, Manufacturing and Construction are also male-dominated, with significantly fewer females.

The Services sector shows a more balanced gender ratio, although males still make up the majority.

From the data, women are more likely to be out of the workforce due to reasons like "Family Duty" and "School". This is particularly evident in the "Family Duty" category, where females far outnumber males, indicating a potential barrier to labor force participation for women.

Unemployment

Unemployment Duration Trends

The visualization shows the changes in unemployment duration in Malaysia from 2017 to 2023. The less than 3 months category has grown significantly, reaching 68% in 2023, indicating that more people are finding jobs quickly compared to earlier years.

The 3 to 6 months category saw a decline from around 30% to 18% in 2023, showing a shift toward shorter unemployment periods. Meanwhile, the 6 months to 1 year category remained steady, around 9-14%, with a slight dip to 9% in 2023.

Long-term unemployment, categorized as more than 1 year, has consistently decreased, making up only 5% of the unemployed in 2023. This suggests improving job market conditions and reduced long-term unemployment.

Work Experience Distribution of Unemployed Individuals by Age Group

The visualization shows that in the younger age groups (15-24), the majority of unemployed individuals have never worked before, with significantly fewer having prior work experience.

In the middle age groups (25-39), the gap between those who have worked and those who haven't begins to narrow.

In the older age groups (40-64), most unemployed individuals have worked before, indicating a shift where older individuals are more likely to have previous work experience, while younger individuals are more likely to have never worked.

Outside Labour Force

Reasons for Non-Participation in the Labour Force

The chart displays the reasons for non-participation in the labour force from 2017 to 2023, ranking the reasons by prevalence.

Family Responsibility and School consistently rank at the top, with School being the dominant reason for non-participation. In 2020, Family Responsibility briefly overtook School, highlighting the influence of caregiving and educational commitments.

Retirement remains in third place, reflecting a stable proportion of retirees. Further Studies and Disabled fluctuate in lower ranks, with Further Studies rising in rank in 2021. Not Interested consistently ranks lowest, indicating it is a less common reason for non-participation.

Overall, the chart shows that non-participation is primarily driven by education and family obligations, with some shifts reflecting changes in societal dynamics.

Gender Distribution of Non-Participation in the Labour Force

The chart illustrates the gender distribution of non-participation in the labour force, highlighting significant trends.

Family Duty heavily impacts females, with 2,984.8 recorded for females compared to 85.2 for males.

In contrast, the School category shows 1,298.7 males and 1,459.3 females, indicating education commitments are slightly more prevalent among females.

The Retired category reveals a higher value for males at 498.2, while Disabled shows 183.4 males versus 70.1 females.

For Further Study and No Interest, males report higher rates, with 60.1 compared to 54 for females in Further Study and 62.8 versus 15.4 in No Interest.

Overall, the chart highlights significant gender disparities in non-participation reasons, with Family Duty and School as major factors for women.
